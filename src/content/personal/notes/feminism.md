Most of us grow up hearing the same tired words used to describe what it means to be a man: *Alpha. Dominant. Strong.* But over time, I’ve come to realize that words like "Alpha" and "Beta" are just labels created by a radical, toxic way of thinking. They reduce human beings to physical traits—height, bone structure, athletic ability.

But a physical measurement is a terrible way to measure a man.

The reality is that men are not all the same. We don’t all fit into the loud, aggressive mold. Yet, society has created an environment where being a bully is somehow considered "cool." It’s seen as a sign of dominance to make fun of someone else without a second thought for their feelings. A true friend, a truly strong person, evaluates the boundaries. They listen. They ask, *"Am I crossing a line? Does this make you uncomfortable?"* But that kind of empathy is rarely rewarded.
Instead, young men are pushed into a performance, and it starts early.

Between the ages of 13 and 25, biology and hormones kick in, and both young men and women begin figuring out what they desire. During this critical window, the definition of "cool" is heavily shaped by what women are perceived to want, and what society tells young men is valuable: money, a specific physique, luxurious things, and a high "body count." Whether intentional or unintentional, this dynamic creates a deeply imbalanced and toxic environment for men.
If you don’t have a girlfriend, you’re looked at as abnormal. If your interests are gaming, reading, or just sitting alone with your thoughts, you are labeled a nerd. You get pushed aside. I’ve even seen guys in my own circles who get good grades but pretend they never study. They hide their effort just to look naturally gifted, and then they mock the guys who learn a little slower and have to work harder. The pressure to fit in is so intense that men will bully other men for not drinking, not smoking, or just for being different.

And here is the hardest part: **There isn’t a word for this specific kind of oppression.**

When men are bullied, marginalized, or treated poorly because they don't fit the material or physical mold, it goes unheard. Men don't speak up because they are afraid of the very friends who are supposed to support them. Friendship shouldn't just be about who has your back in a fight or who lends you money; it should be about empathy and understanding. But because we lack the vocabulary for male vulnerability, men stay silent.

This brings me to feminism.
When I look at true feminism, I don’t see a movement that should be strictly about women; I see a movement about humanity.

People often ask, *"What would happen if the world were run by women?"* Some assume it would be a guaranteed utopia. But power has no gender. Money and power can corrupt anyone equally. If the world were run by women, but they were driven by capitalism, self-centeredness, and revenge against men, it wouldn't be a better world. True feminism isn't about replacing a male-dominated hierarchy with a female-dominated one. It requires a symphony—men and women working together.

Somewhere along the way, we forgot what feminism is actually fighting for. Many people, especially in the developed world, have reduced it to a checklist of professional metrics or identical outcomes. Sometimes, the basic freedoms—like the right to free speech and safety, which are still fought for in third-world countries—are taken for granted by those demanding power rather than true equality.
Men and women are biologically different. We cannot do all the exact same things, and that is okay. Equality doesn't mean being physically or professionally identical. Equality is freedom. It is being respected. It is living with the consequences of your actions.
We have seen the atrocities of a world ruled by dominance, cruelty, and the toxic definition of what it means to "be a man." We've been taught that strength means controlling others.
But I believe true strength is having an open mind. It is welcoming different perspectives because, ultimately, we are all just human beings trying to do our best. Even the people who do harm often believe they are doing the right thing from their own warped perspective.

We don't need to fight against each other. The woke movements, the feminist movements, the silent men struggling to find their place—we are all ultimately fighting for the same thing: to be seen, to be respected, and to be allowed to exist without having to perform.
We just need to start working together to build a society where "being a man" simply means being a good, empathetic human.
